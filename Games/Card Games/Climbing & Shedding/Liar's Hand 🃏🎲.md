
# Liar's Hand

*A Liar's Dice-style bluffing game with cards — for 3–8 players*

---

## 🎯 Objective

Read the table, push the bid, or call **Liar!** at the right moment. Win the hand by making the correct challenge — or by surviving when someone calls you out.

---

## 🃏 The Game in Brief

> Each player holds **6 secret cards**. **Jacks, Queens, and Kings are wild** and count toward any declared rank.
>
> On your turn, either **raise the bid** (claiming more cards of a rank exist across all hands) or call **Liar!** on the current bid.
>
> When **Liar!** is called, everyone reveals their hands. Count every card that matches the declared rank **or** is a wild figure.
>
> - If the table has **at least** as many matching cards as the last bid claimed → the caller was wrong; **everyone else wins** the hand.
> - If the table has **fewer** → the caller was right; **the caller wins** the hand alone.

---

## 📦 Components

- **2 standard 52-card decks** shuffled together (you can also play with Jokers)

---

## 👥 Players

**2–6 players.** Best with 3–4.

---

## 🛠️ Setup

1. Shuffle the deck(s).
2. Deal **6 cards** face down to each player.
3. Players look at their hands in secret.
4. Choose a starting player (deal a card for high card, or rotate each hand).

---

## 📣 Making a Bid

A bid has two parts: a **quantity** and a **rank**.

- **Quantity** — how many cards of that rank you claim exist across **all hands combined** (including wilds).
- **Rank** — one of 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 7, 8, 9, 10, or A.

### Opening bid

The first player of the hand must open with a bet of any quantity and rank.

Examples of opening bids:
- *"One 2"* 
- *"Three 10s"*

### Raising a bid

Each new bid must be **strictly higher in quantity** than the last **by at least one**. The rank does not matter.

Example of sequence of bids
- *"One 7"* -> *"Four 3s"* -> *"Five 3s"* -> *"Six 8s" -> "Seven 7s"*
- *"Two 7s"* -> *"Three 6s"* -> *"Four 9s"* -> *"Five Aces"*

---

## 🚨 Winning the Hand

Instead of raising, you may call ****Liar!*** on the current bid.

1. All players reveal their hands simultaneously.
2. All players count every card that is either the **declared rank**, or a **wild** (J, Q, K) by placing them into the middle
3. Compare the count to the last bid's quantity.

| Result                                          | Outcome                                                                         |
| ----------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Count **≥** bid quantity                        | ✅The bid was **true**. The caller loses — **every other player wins** the hand. |
| Count **<** bid quantity                        | ❌ The bid was a **lie**. The caller wins the hand **alone**.                    |
| ⚠️ There are no cards of that rank (only wilds) | ❌ The bid was a **lie**. You need **at least one natural**.                     |

---

## 🏆 Scoring & Winning

Play multiple hands until someone reaches an agreed target (e.g. **3 points**).

- **Correct Liar! call** → caller gains **1 point**.
- **Wrong Liar! call** → each other player gains **1 point**.

Tiebreaker: most recent hand winner, or play one more sudden-death hand.

## 📋 Example Hand (4 players)

**Hands (secret):**

| Player | Cards |
|---|---|
| A | 7♠, 7♥, K♣, 4♦, 2♠ |
| B | J♠, 9♣, 9♥, 3♦, 5♠ |
| C | 7♦, Q♥, A♣, 6♠, 8♥ |
| D | 4♣, 4♥, 10♠, 3♣, K♦ |

**Bidding:**

1. **A** opens: *"Three 7s"*
2. **B**: *"Four 9s"*
3. **C**: *"Five 9s"*
4. **D**: *"Six 7s"*
5. **A**: **Liar!**

**Reveal:** Actual 7s are 7♠, 7♥, 7♦ (three). Wilds that count: K♣, J♠, Q♥, K♦ (four). **Total: seven.**

The bid was *"Six 7s"* — seven ≥ six, so the bid was true. **B, C, and D each win the hand.** A called wrong, and everyone else wins

## 💡 Tips

- Use probability to estimate the number of wilds in play.
    
- You don't have to bet small every time. Bet big to ensure that, when it's your turn to act, you can call *liar* with confidence.
    
- The *mulligan* gives away precious information. The cards that you choose to keep are probably either *pairs* or *wilds*. If you start bluffing immediately by keeping bad cards in hand, you may throw off your opponents, as they will be more confident in making big bets.
    
- Early *liar* calls are risky when few cards are accounted for; late calls are about reading who's bluffing on quantity.
    
- Throw off your opponents by insisting on a rank that you don't have, especially when you have many wilds to back you up.
    
- With two decks you will draw no pairs (ignoring the wilds) about 43% of the times.

---

## Variants

- Different number of cards in hand
    
- If you call a liar correctly, you start the subsequent hands with one extra card in hand, which gives you extra information.
    
- The Mulligan 
	- Before the opening bid begins, players may discard any number of cards face down, then draw back the same number of cards.
