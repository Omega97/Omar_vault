
### Commentary by Professional Shogi Player Shota Chida (7-dan) on the 100-game match between "AlphaZero vs Elmo"

Shota Chida 7-dan (then 6-dan), famous as a professional player highly knowledgeable about shogi engines, provided commentary on the game records of the 100-game match between AlphaZero and Elmo regarding the strongest shogi software "AlphaZero." The key takeaways can be summarized in the following three points.

**Point 1: Initially, it keeps the Bishop diagonal closed and only pushes the Rook pawn.**

It is said that "AlphaZero's first move is always ▲P-2f (▲2六歩), and the second move is always △P-8d (△8四歩)." Since AlphaZero does not reference established openings (*joseki*) or professional game records, it explores what to play on the first and second moves from a completely blank slate with no prior information. As a result, in the very beginning, it was apparently playing moves like ▲P-7f (▲7六歩) and △P-3d (△3四歩), which open the Bishop diagonal, quite normally.

However, AlphaZero became stronger than Elmo in just two hours, and around that time, it stopped playing △P-3d on the second move in response to ▲P-7f or ▲P-2f. It seems this is because a realization was born within the software that "if you engage in *Yokodori* (capturing the surrendered Rook pawn), it leads to a disadvantage." Specifically, it judges that if Black plays *Yokodori* and the opponent counters with the "Aono-ryu" variation—which is currently played even by professionals—the position becomes difficult. In other words, it seems AlphaZero's conclusion is that "*Yokodori* is a dead end / no longer viable."

Then, what about the first move ▲P-7f? The progression of first move ▲P-7f followed by △P-8d on the second move was still common even at the two-hour mark, which was when △P-3d drastically decreased. However, after about 5 hours of self-play, the first move ▲P-7f also started to decrease. As for the reason, Chida 6-dan's view was, "Isn't the software highly evaluating the exchange of the Rook pawn?" Indeed, if you play ▲P-7f first, whether it leads to a Bishop Exchange (*Kakugawari*) or a Fortress (*Yagura*) opening, you cannot immediately exchange the Rook pawn. AlphaZero doesn't open the Bishop diagonal, so it plays a lot of Double Static Rook (*Aigakari*) games. Chida 6-dan predicted that professionals might also be influenced by this, and Double Static Rook games might become more common in the future.

**Point 2: AlphaZero doesn't mind a Central King (*Nakadan Gyoku*)**

The second point is, "AlphaZero does not mind having its King in the central ranks." Even if AlphaZero's castle is broken and its King is driven upwards, it is very good at dodging the opponent's attacks and slipping away smoothly, making it hard to defeat. Rather than just enduring it, it even seems to prefer moving its King into the central ranks. 

Chida 6-dan mentioned that this kind of King maneuvering (*Tama-sabaki*) is similar to Shingo Sawada 6-dan. Names like Masayuki Itoyama 8-dan, Takuya Yamazaki 8-dan, and Yasumitsu Sato 9-dan were also mentioned in the broadcast comments, and AlphaZero seems to excel at King maneuvering that reminds one of those players. Prioritizing balance over the hardness (safety) of the King has traditionally been a characteristic of shogi engines, and professional players have been influenced by this. With the appearance of AlphaZero, which has an even stronger tendency to move its King around, the trend of prioritizing balance and attacking over the King's safety seems likely to become even stronger in future professional shogi.

**Point 3: Professional Shogi is Already in the Post-Engine Era**

The third point is, "Professional shogi is already in the state of having been influenced by engines." While explaining the AlphaZero game records, Chida 6-dan said, "It probably looks not so different from current professional shogi, doesn't it?" The endgame play, such as the King running away, is unique to engines, but the opening maneuvering resembles professional play. However, this is because "current professional shogi is already in the aftermath of learning from engines." 

In fact, the method of becoming strong without referencing established openings or human game records existed even before the advent of this AlphaZero. And from around 2016, the opening tactics born from such engines have had a massive impact on professional shogi players. Specifically, the ▲4h Gold (▲4八金) formation in Bishop Exchange, the Quick Attack against Left Mino, the ▲6g Left Gold (▲6七金左) formation in Fortress, and the Hanging Wing (*Gangui*) opening. All of these are strategies that professional players started playing in recent years, and they are all things that humans adopted from engine shogi.
